Real-Time Supply-Balancing Agent

A LangGraph-based agent that watches live ride-supply imbalances across a major mobility platform and triggers targeted driver incentives — replacing a rules-based system that was overspending by design.

01The problem

Ride-hailing supply rarely matches demand evenly across a city. The legacy approach was a hand-tuned rule engine that pushed blanket incentives whenever wait times rose — effective at fixing the symptom, but expensive and slow to adapt to local conditions like weather, events, or shift patterns.

The opportunity was a system that could reason about why supply was tight in a specific zone, decide whether an incentive would actually help, and choose the smallest intervention that would close the gap.

02The approach

I designed a multi-step agent in LangGraph that decomposed the problem into perception, hypothesis, and action stages. The agent ingests a continuous stream of zone-level supply/demand signals, builds a short-horizon forecast, queries contextual signals (weather, calendar events, historic shift patterns), and reasons through candidate interventions before committing.

Each decision is logged with the agent's chain of reasoning so ops teams can audit why a campaign was launched and override when needed.

03What I built

  • Stateful LangGraph agent with separate nodes for forecasting, hypothesis generation, intervention selection, and human handoff
  • FastAPI service layer with a feature store for low-latency context retrieval
  • A guardrail layer that bounded incentive spend per zone, per hour, with auto-rollback on anomaly detection
  • An observability dashboard showing every agent decision, its reasoning trace, and the resulting market outcome
  • Evaluation harness for offline backtesting against historic supply events before each model upgrade

06What I learned

The hardest part of agentic systems isn't getting the agent to reason — it's bounding what it's allowed to do. Most of the engineering time went into guardrails, observability, and rollback paths, not prompts. The lesson I'd carry to the next project: design the failure modes before designing the happy path.
