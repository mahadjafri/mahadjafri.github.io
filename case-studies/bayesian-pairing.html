Bayesian Pairing Models for Contact-Center Routing

Hierarchical Bayesian models that match incoming customers to the agent most likely to resolve their call well — deployed across telecom clients globally, driving an estimated $20M in incremental revenue.

01The problem

Contact centers historically routed calls based on availability — first idle agent gets the next caller. The underlying intuition that "some agent-customer pairings just work better" was widely held but rarely modeled. The challenge was estimating those pairing effects from sparse, noisy data: most agent-customer pairs have never occurred.

02The approach

I built hierarchical Bayesian models that pooled information across customer segments and agent attributes — letting the model make calibrated predictions about pairings it had never seen by borrowing strength from related ones.

Posterior distributions, not point estimates, drove the routing decision: pairs with similar expected outcomes but different uncertainty were treated differently, which let the system explore promising-but-unproven pairings without sacrificing short-term outcomes.

03What I built

  • Hierarchical Bayesian model architecture with partial pooling across customer and agent features
  • Production inference service emitting routing recommendations within tight real-time SLAs
  • Counterfactual evaluation framework for A/B-testing pairing strategies against rotation-based controls
  • Client-facing reporting that decomposed lift into pairing-driven vs. baseline effects

06What I learned

Calibration matters more than peak accuracy in routing problems. A model that says "this pairing is great" with high confidence can lock the system into the wrong policy; one that quantifies its uncertainty lets the routing engine balance exploitation with exploration. The Bayesian framing wasn't aesthetic — it was operational.
